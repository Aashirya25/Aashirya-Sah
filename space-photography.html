First quarter moon

Captured on the 6th of January 🌗 if you look closely, you'll notice a slight blue glow around the edge. That's not a camera glitch — it's actually chromatic aberration, a common optical effect where different colors of light don't focus perfectly together. It happens because lenses bend blue light more than red light.

Jupiter and its Moons

Captured on the 10th of January. Jupiter with its Galilean moons lined up. Really cool to catch a whole mini solar system in one shot.

Caught Jupiter again

Captured on the 11th of January. Those four tiny dots lined up beside it are its four largest moons: Io, Europa, Ganymede, and Callisto. They're called the Galilean moons — visible even through small telescopes because they orbit in a straight line along Jupiter's equator. The glow and starburst around the planet come from the camera lens reacting to how bright Jupiter is compared to its moons.

Mars!

Spotted this glowing red dot through my telescope and instantly knew it had to be Mars. The reddish hue is its natural color, thanks to all the iron oxide (rust!) on its surface.

The Red Planet! Our future home?

This glowing dot in my telescope is hiding some wild stuff. It's home to the largest volcano in the solar system, Olympus Mons. Scientists found signs of ancient riverbeds, there might be frozen water underground at the poles. Crazy to believe this little red dot might've once been alive.

Mars is that you?

Not Mars — this is actually the Moon 🔥🌕 The red glow came from Earth's atmosphere bending the light while I was photographing through the telescope. You can still see the craters along the edge, right where the light meets the dark — that line is called the terminator. The deep orange tint caused by Rayleigh scattering

A light Show! (use chrome to view video)

What you're seeing isn't the star changing shape — it's the atmosphere, my telescope, and my camera sensor all playing optical tricks. Between atmospheric turbulence, chromatic aberration, and a very bright star, I ended up catching this rainbow explosion in the eyepiece. It's chaotic, but kind of beautiful.

Cant forget about the sun!

This might just look like a blurry white ball… But it's actually the Sun, and those tiny dark spots? Sunspots — cooler, magnetically active regions on the surface caused by twisted magnetic fields. I'm basically looking at giant magnetic storms on a star, 150 million km away. Also: don't worry, I used a solar filter. I love my eyes.

My set up

It's nothing fancy — just a curious teenager with access to a balcony from my room. I don't have a professional camera, so I use my iPhone, and the tripod helps keep it steady. I still have to adjust it every so often because of the Earth's rotation — the Moon and Sun slowly drift out of frame, so I move the telescope to follow them. Not the most high-tech setup… but it works. And I've learned more from doing this than I ever expected (check out my blog to know more).
